About the Desk

One desk, four signals, and a standing refusal to predict.

ChartHorizon is a one-desk futures operation. The Weekly Tape is its public page: market notes written from the same end-of-day data the desk reads every evening — nothing more, and deliberately nothing less. The voice is written in the tape-reading lineage of Jesse Livermore, the trader who insisted that the market itself, not anyone's opinion of it, tells you where the line of least resistance runs. Hence the line on the masthead: positioning, not predictions.

The four lights

Every market on this page is read the same way, against four signals: the season (the time-of-year tendency), the producers' positioning (the commercial net from the CFTC's Commitments of Traders report), the hedging program (which way the commercial book has been moving), and the term structure (premium or discount, front month against the next). When all four burn the same color, the market is showing its full hand — those are the setups worth writing about; the rest get left alone. On the FX Map a fifth light joins the read: the interest-rate bias across the eight majors.

A word of candor the marketing departments leave out: a full hand is a permission, not a promise. Positioning tells you who is leaning on a market; it does not tell you when the market will start to care.

The editions

The Weekly Tape — weekly notes on the markets where all four signals line up, written from the prior week's close.
The Hedgers' Ledger — after each CFTC Commitments-of-Traders release: where the commercial book sits at the edge of its multi-year range.
The FX Map — daily: the currency-strength scoreboard for the eight majors and the policy-rate backdrop behind it.

Everything is computed from end-of-day futures and CFTC data by ChartHorizon's own research dashboard; this site publishes that dashboard's output, set in type.

What this is not

Not a signal service, and not financial advice. There are no trade calls, no track-record theater, and no premium tier with secret arrows. The notes are educational: they show a method for reading positioning, and they show their work. Futures trading involves substantial risk of loss, the data here is end-of-day rather than real-time, and anyone promising you a live edge from a free webpage is selling something. Read the argument, check it against your own, and make your own decisions.

The name on the door

The desk publishes under the masthead, not a byline. That is a deliberate choice in a corner of the internet crowded with confident faces: an anonymous page cannot borrow authority from a résumé, so the work has to stand on its own — and you should hold it to exactly that standard.
